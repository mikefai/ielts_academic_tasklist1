Listening

6 question types · Audio plays ONCE only · 40 questions

L1
Multiple Choice
MCQ
What & WhyA question stem plus 3–4 options (A–C or A–D), or a "choose TWO" variant with 5+ options. Tests specific information, main ideas, speaker attitudes, and implied meanings. The recording almost never uses the exact words of the correct option — it paraphrases instead.
  • 1Read ALL questions during the 30-second pre-section pause. Underline the key concept in each stem so you know exactly what to listen for.
  • 2Predict the topic of each option before the audio begins. This primes active listening rather than reactive scrambling.
  • 3Listen for paraphrase, not word-match. Correct answers are almost always semantic equivalents of what the speaker says, not copies.
  • 4Eliminate actively. Speakers deliberately mention all options — but contradict or correct two of them. Cross out eliminated options in real time.
  • 5Watch for mid-sentence changes. "Let's do Tuesday — actually, Wednesday works better." The LAST statement is always correct.
  • 6For "choose TWO" questions, treat each option as an independent yes/no decision. Don't look for a pre-set pair.
  • 7Mark your best guess and move on immediately if you miss an answer. Hesitation causes you to miss the next question too.
Common Mistakes
  • Choosing the first option you hear that matches — distractors are placed early deliberately.
  • Picking based on a single keyword match rather than overall meaning.
  • Not reading ahead during section gaps, leaving yourself unprepared.
Use all 30 seconds before each section to read ALL questions for that section, not just the first. Transfer answers in the 10-minute period at the end — don't write on the answer sheet mid-listening.
L2
Matching
Match
What & WhyA two-column task. Left column: a list of items (people, events, facilities). Right column: a bank of options. Some options can be reused; some may not be used at all. Common in Sections 3 & 4.
  • 1Read BOTH columns in full during the pre-section pause. Understand what each bank option means — don't just memorise letters.
  • 2Predict the type of information needed. If the bank contains adjectives (expensive, innovative), expect evaluative language in the recording.
  • 3Left-column items follow audio order. Use this to pace yourself — you know roughly when each item will be addressed.
  • 4Listen for contrast language: "Unlike X, Y is…", "The problem with… is…", "What makes this different is…"
  • 5Don't commit too early. A speaker may initially mention one option then correct or expand with a different one.
  • 6Check reuse rules. If the instructions say "you may use any letter more than once," the same bank option may correctly match multiple items.
Common Mistakes
  • Missing the instruction about whether options can be reused — this changes your entire approach.
  • Matching too quickly on a superficial keyword before the speaker finishes their point.
  • Ignoring options that seem unlikely — distractors are designed to seem plausible at first.
During the pre-section pause, scan both columns equally rather than reading any single item deeply. If torn between two options, jot both and decide during the 10-minute transfer.
L3
Plan / Map / Diagram Labelling
Visual
What & WhyA visual image (floor plan, town map, technical diagram) with numbered blank labels. You write the correct word(s) or select a letter from a box based on what you hear. Requires connecting spoken language to spatial representations.
  • 1Spend 20–25 seconds of your pre-section time studying the image. Identify existing labels — these are your orientation anchors.
  • 2Establish a sense of direction. Note compass points (N/S/E/W) or relative positions (left/right/opposite/adjacent).
  • 3Underline existing labels on the map. The speaker will use these as reference points ("turn left at the fountain") — follow the route in real time.
  • 4Write words exactly as spoken. Correct spelling is required. If you hear a proper noun (e.g. a room name), write it carefully.
  • 5Follow audio sequence. Numbered items almost always appear in the order the speaker describes the space — don't skip ahead.
  • 6For letter-matching variants, check all box options first, then eliminate as the recording progresses.
Common Mistakes
  • Losing your place on the map because you committed too much attention to writing one label.
  • Spelling errors on clearly pronounced words — these cost marks unnecessarily.
  • Confusing directional language: "to the left of the entrance" vs "on the left as you exit" are different.
If you miss a label, don't trace back — mentally move to the next numbered blank immediately. The investment of 20+ seconds studying the image before listening pays large dividends.
L4
Form / Note / Table / Flow-chart Completion
Fill
What & WhyAn organised visual (booking form, notes, table, flow-chart, or summary) with numbered blanks. You write words from the recording. Very common in Sections 1 & 2. Word limit is strictly stated (e.g., NO MORE THAN TWO WORDS AND/OR A NUMBER).
  • 1Read the WHOLE task before the audio begins. Understand the context and predict the type of answer for each blank (name, number, date, adjective, noun phrase).
  • 2Use existing text as a grammatical guide. Words around the blank tell you what type of word is needed (article before? plural needed?).
  • 3Listen for paraphrase in the question, but copy answers word-for-word. The task text may paraphrase the recording, but your answer must use the speaker's exact words.
  • 4Anticipate category-specific language. Forms: names, phone numbers, dates, prices. Flow-charts: sequence verbs and process nouns. Tables: comparisons.
  • 5Numbers and dates are often spelled out in audio. "Two hundred and thirty-five" = 235. Decide whether to write digit or word based on context.
  • 6Check word count before transferring. "The large conference room" is three words — if limit is ONE WORD, write only the most informative word.
Common Mistakes
  • Exceeding the word limit — this automatically loses the mark even if your answer is correct.
  • Mishearing similar-sounding numbers: "13" vs "30", "15" vs "50". Use context to verify.
  • Skipping ahead and losing track of your position when the audio moves faster than expected.
Section 1 is almost always this type and is the easiest section — use it to build confidence and bank easy marks. If you miss a blank, write your best guess and move forward.
L5
Sentence Completion
Sentence
What & Why5–10 standalone sentences, each with one or two numbered gaps. Sentences summarise key information from the recording. Unlike Summary Completion, each item is independent. You write exact words from the audio.
  • 1Read each FULL sentence during the pre-section pause. Understand the sentence's overall meaning — not just the words around the blank.
  • 2Predict the grammatical category of the answer. "The _____ was reported" needs a noun. "They decided to _____" needs a verb infinitive.
  • 3Use the sentence beginning as a locating tool. The stem will appear (possibly paraphrased) in the audio just before the answer is spoken.
  • 4Write the exact word from the recording. Do not paraphrase or change tense. If the speaker says "extended," write "extended," not "prolonged."
  • 5Check grammatical sense. If your completed sentence reads unnaturally, you likely have the wrong word.
  • 6Maintain order awareness. Sentences follow audio order — use this to predict approximately when each answer arrives.
Common Mistakes
  • Changing word form (e.g. writing "organisation" when the grammatical slot needs "organised").
  • Writing too many words because you weren't tracking the stated limit.
  • Missing answers by fixating on an earlier sentence — always keep moving forward.
Allow roughly 30–40 seconds per question as a mental benchmark. Use the 10-minute transfer time to check the grammar fit of every completed sentence.
L6
Short-Answer Questions
Short
What & WhyWh-questions (who, what, where, when, why, how many) about specific factual details. Answers are 1–3 words and/or a number. Most common in Sections 1 & 4. The question tells you exactly what type of detail to listen for.
  • 1Read all questions during the pre-section pause and underline the question word (what, where, how many). This defines your listening target.
  • 2Convert the question into a listening focus. "What is the maximum weight?" → listen for a weight measurement.
  • 3Listen for signal phrases that precede answers: "The limit is…", "You can find it at…", "The deadline is…"
  • 4Write precise words from the recording. "What time does the tour begin?" + speaker says "half past nine" → write "9.30" or "half past nine."
  • 5Stay ahead of the audio. After writing each answer, immediately shift attention to the NEXT question.
  • 6Check spelling. Dates, numbers, and common nouns must be spelled correctly. Blank answers score zero — a guess might be right.
Common Mistakes
  • Writing a full sentence instead of the short noun-phrase answer — you never gain extra marks this way.
  • Spelling mistakes on simple words (dates, numbers, common objects).
  • Confusing similar-sounding numbers and dates in the recording.
Each question is answered in about 5–10 seconds of audio — pace is very fast. Stay continuously engaged. If you miss one, write your best guess and move on immediately.

Reading

11 question types · 3 passages · 60 min · Write directly on answer sheet

R1
Multiple Choice
MCQ
What & WhyA question or incomplete sentence with 3–4 options (A–D), or multi-answer variants (choose TWO from A–E). Tests detailed comprehension, main ideas, writer's purpose, and implied meanings. All four options are designed to sound plausible — only one is precisely supported by the text.
  • 1Read the question carefully and identify exactly what is asked: a detail, main idea, implication, or writer's attitude.
  • 2Locate the relevant passage section first, then evaluate options. Use key words from the question to scan for the right paragraph.
  • 3Read the relevant section THOROUGHLY — not just the sentence with the keyword, but 2–3 surrounding sentences for context.
  • 4Evaluate ALL options before selecting. The correct answer is precisely supported; distractors are too extreme, partially correct, or opposite in meaning.
  • 5Eliminate options that go beyond the text. Answers requiring external knowledge or unsupported inferences are always wrong.
  • 6Beware of extreme language (always, never, all, the only) in options — rarely correct unless the text uses the same absolute language.
Common Mistakes
  • Choosing based on familiarity — what "sounds right" from general knowledge rather than the text.
  • Not reading the full relevant section — an option may look right based on one sentence but be contradicted elsewhere.
  • Spending too long on this type — budget maximum 90 seconds per question.
Target no more than 90 seconds per question. If a question is consuming too much time, mark your best guess and move on — return at the end if time allows.
R2
Identifying Information (True / False / Not Given)
T/F/NG
What & WhyEach statement is either TRUE (passage confirms it), FALSE (passage contradicts it), or NOT GIVEN (information is entirely absent). This type tests literal comprehension of factual information — NOT opinions. The difference between FALSE and NOT GIVEN is the most tested distinction.
  • 1Internalise the three definitions before your exam: TRUE = confirmed; FALSE = actively contradicted; NOT GIVEN = simply absent. Not finding something ≠ FALSE.
  • 2Locate the relevant section by scanning for key nouns or proper names from the statement.
  • 3Read that section carefully — compare the statement to the text word-by-word if necessary.
  • 4For NOT GIVEN: ask "Is this information anywhere in this passage?" Search twice before committing. Do NOT guess FALSE just because you can't find it.
  • 5Watch for partial matches — a statement may use a word from the text but describe a different relationship (cause vs. effect, more vs. fewer).
  • 6Statements follow text order — use this to move efficiently through the passage without re-reading entire sections.
  • 7Never use outside knowledge. Even if you know a fact to be true in reality, if the passage doesn't state it, the answer is NOT GIVEN.
Common Mistakes
  • Confusing FALSE and NOT GIVEN — the most common error in Reading. FALSE requires active contradiction; NOT GIVEN means total absence.
  • Reading too quickly and missing qualifiers (some, most, rarely) that change a statement's meaning.
  • Choosing TRUE based on partial evidence without verifying the FULL statement is supported.
Aim for 60–75 seconds per statement. NOT GIVEN is often the fastest to confirm — once you've scanned the passage twice without finding the information, commit and move on.
R3
Identifying Writer's Views (Yes / No / Not Given)
Y/N/NG
What & WhySuperficially similar to T/F/NG but fundamentally different: here you evaluate the WRITER'S OPINIONS, not facts. YES = writer's view agrees with the statement. NO = writer's view contradicts it. NOT GIVEN = writer doesn't express a view on this. The key skill is detecting the author's stance.
  • 1Switch your reading mode to "opinion-detection." Look for stance markers: "it is clear that…", "arguably…", "this suggests…", "critics fail to…", "the evidence indicates…"
  • 2Distinguish facts from views. "Carbon levels rose in 2005" is a fact. "This rise was entirely avoidable" is an opinion.
  • 3Locate the relevant section using key nouns from the statement, then read the FULL paragraph for context.
  • 4Ask two questions: (a) Does the writer address this topic? If no → NOT GIVEN. (b) Does the writer agree or disagree with the statement's claim? → YES or NO.
  • 5Watch for indirect disagreement. Writers often express NO by criticising, undermining, or questioning a position rather than explicitly rejecting it.
  • 6Pay attention to hedging language. Implied views may sometimes lean toward NOT GIVEN if the writer is non-committal.
Common Mistakes
  • Treating this like T/F/NG and looking for facts instead of opinions — leads to systematically wrong answers.
  • Projecting your own opinion or general knowledge onto the writer's position.
  • Choosing YES or NO based on fragments without reading the full argument context.
Budget 75–90 seconds per item — identifying opinions requires deeper reading than fact-finding. Read the passage introduction carefully first to understand the writer's overall stance.
R4
Matching Information
Match
What & WhyA list of pieces of information (facts, examples, explanations) must be matched to the correct paragraph (A, B, C…). Paragraphs can be used more than once; some may not be used at all. The task rewards efficient scanning, not linear reading.
  • 1Read ALL information items first before going to the passage — know what you're hunting for before you search.
  • 2Do NOT read the entire passage first. This task rewards scanning, not linear reading.
  • 3Scan each paragraph for the concept — not the exact wording. Items almost always paraphrase the passage.
  • 4Work item by item. Take one item, identify its key concept(s), scan the full passage, confirm by reading closely.
  • 5Know paragraph structure. Key examples appear mid-paragraph; main claims appear at the start or end.
  • 6Check if reuse is allowed — it is always explicitly stated in the instructions and it's intentional.
Common Mistakes
  • Matching based on a keyword alone without confirming the full meaning matches the item description.
  • Spending too long scanning — set a 2-minute limit per item and move on if stuck.
  • Assuming each paragraph is used exactly once — the task allows multiple uses.
This is one of the most time-consuming Reading types. Budget 2 minutes per item, max 12–14 minutes for a 6-item set. Do easier items (specific, findable information) first.
R5
Matching Headings
Headings
What & Why6–9 headings (i, ii, iii…) are given. Assign the most appropriate heading to each paragraph or section. Always more headings than paragraphs — some won't be used. Tests ability to identify the main idea of a paragraph, not supporting details.
  • 1Read ALL headings first before touching the passage. Understand each one and mentally group similar-sounding headings to distinguish them.
  • 2Eliminate clearly wrong headings after skimming each paragraph — narrows your choices rapidly.
  • 3Focus on the topic sentence (usually first or last sentence of each paragraph) — it typically captures the main idea the heading should match.
  • 4Match main ideas, not details. A heading must represent the paragraph's dominant point, not a supporting example mentioned within it.
  • 5Use process of elimination. After confidently matching 3–4 paragraphs, use remaining headings to narrow choices for harder ones.
  • 6Headings do NOT follow the order of the list. You may use heading vii before heading ii — order is irrelevant.
Common Mistakes
  • Matching based on a single word that appears in both heading and paragraph — the heading must capture the paragraph's full main idea.
  • Spending too long on one difficult paragraph — do easy matches first and return.
  • Reading the entire passage word-for-word before attempting — skimming is far more efficient.
Target 90 seconds per paragraph. If this task appears before the passage, do it first — understanding the paragraph structure helps all subsequent question types on that passage.
R6
Matching Features
Features
What & WhyStatements must be matched to features in a box (researchers, theories, dates, countries, organisations). Options can be reused. Tests ability to identify specific information attributed to particular sources — common in scientific or historical texts.
  • 1Read the features in the box first and understand what each represents (a person's name, a country, a theory label).
  • 2Read each statement and identify what attribute of the feature you need to match — their finding, their criticism, their location.
  • 3Build a mental map of where each feature appears in the passage. Scan for proper nouns and note which paragraphs mention each one.
  • 4Answers do NOT follow text order — statements may refer to features scattered throughout the passage.
  • 5Pay attention to attribution language: "According to Smith…", "Jones argued that…", "Unlike earlier theories, X proposed…"
  • 6Reuse is allowed and intentional — if the same researcher is mentioned for two different statements, both may correctly match to that researcher.
Common Mistakes
  • Assuming each feature is used only once — always check the instructions.
  • Scanning only for the feature name without reading surrounding context to verify the connection.
  • Confusing features with similar descriptions (two researchers sharing similar but distinct views).
Build a quick reference list (e.g. "Smith = paragraphs C, D; Jones = paragraph B") before answering — this saves time. Budget 90 seconds per statement.
R7
Matching Sentence Endings
Endings
What & WhySentence stems are given in a numbered list. A separate box contains possible endings (A, B, C…) — always more endings than stems. Match each stem to its correct ending to form an accurate, complete sentence. Both grammatical sense AND semantic accuracy are required.
  • 1Read ALL stems AND all endings before going to the passage. Understand what each full combination would mean.
  • 2Predict possible completions for each stem before looking at options — reduces the influence of distractors.
  • 3Stems follow text order — use this to scan the passage efficiently from section to section.
  • 4Ensure BOTH grammatical coherence AND semantic accuracy. The completed sentence must make grammatical sense AND accurately reflect the passage.
  • 5Use elimination actively. Some endings can be ruled out for certain stems based on grammar alone (e.g., a stem ending in "are" needs a plural noun).
  • 6Having unused endings is normal — don't panic if one is left over; it confirms you've matched correctly elsewhere.
Common Mistakes
  • Forcing a grammatical match without verifying the semantic accuracy against the passage.
  • Choosing endings that are partly correct — the ENTIRE completed sentence must be fully supported by the text.
  • Not doing your easiest matches first — eliminate obvious endings to simplify remaining choices.
Budget 90 seconds per stem. Do confident matches first — eliminating used endings simplifies the remaining choices significantly.
R8
Sentence Completion
Sentence
What & WhyIncomplete sentences with one or two gaps. Fill each gap with words taken DIRECTLY from the passage. Unlike the listening version, you can re-read as needed. Word limits apply (usually NO MORE THAN TWO WORDS). The task text typically paraphrases the passage, but your answer uses the passage's exact words.
  • 1Read the full incomplete sentence to understand its meaning before searching the passage.
  • 2Identify the grammatical requirement of the gap. Surrounding words constrain the answer type (noun, adjective, verb, adverb).
  • 3Scan the passage for paraphrased language from the sentence stem — your answer uses the passage's exact words, not your own.
  • 4Copy the answer WORD-FOR-WORD from the passage. Paraphrasing costs you the mark.
  • 5Count words carefully. Articles (a, an, the) and prepositions count as words.
  • 6Verify the completed sentence is grammatically correct — if it reads unnaturally, re-read the passage section with your proposed answer inserted.
Common Mistakes
  • Changing word form (e.g. "significant" → "significantly") — use the exact form from the passage.
  • Writing more words than the limit — even if the extra word is correct, you lose the mark.
  • Choosing words nearby in the passage that don't specifically answer the gap.
Sentences follow text order — use this to move efficiently. Budget 60–75 seconds per gap. Generally faster than inference-based tasks.
R9
Summary / Note / Table / Flow-chart Completion
Fill
What & WhyA condensed version of part of the passage — as prose summary, notes, table, or flow-chart — contains numbered gaps. Candidates either select words from a given word bank or extract directly from the passage. Tests identification of main points and supporting details across a passage section.
  • 1Identify which section of the passage the summary relates to before reading it in detail — the title or introductory line usually tells you. This narrows your search zone dramatically.
  • 2For word-bank variants, read all options first and eliminate obviously wrong ones before scanning the passage.
  • 3For passage-extraction variants, use surrounding words as syntactic clues to identify the word type needed.
  • 4Read the passage section carefully — summaries are paraphrased, so your job is to decode the paraphrase and find the original word(s).
  • 5Do not use words from outside the passage (or outside the box for word-bank variants). Even if you "know" what belongs there, use only what is provided.
  • 6Check logical coherence — the completed summary should read as a coherent, accurate account of the passage section.
Common Mistakes
  • Using your own words when extraction from the passage is required — always loses the mark.
  • Missing the word limit — even with a word bank, the gap may only accommodate ONE word.
  • Failing to read the full summary first — without context, choices are essentially random.
Invest 30 seconds locating the relevant passage section before answering — then 60–75 seconds per gap. Finding the right section quickly is the key time-saving move.
R10
Diagram Label Completion
Diagram
What & WhyA diagram, cross-section, illustration, or technical drawing with numbered blank labels. Fill each blank with word(s) from the passage. Often describes a physical object, biological structure, or technical process. Requires close, precise reading of the relevant passage section.
  • 1Study the diagram carefully BEFORE finding the passage section. Understand what the image depicts and what existing labels tell you.
  • 2Identify which passage paragraph describes this diagram — signalled by explicit references ("Figure 1," "as illustrated below") or descriptions of the same object.
  • 3Read that passage section THOROUGHLY — this task requires close reading because spatial/structural descriptions need precise comprehension.
  • 4Match each numbered blank's position to the diagram part it refers to, then find the relevant word in the passage.
  • 5Use existing labels as anchors — if one part is already labelled "valve," find "valve" in the passage and read around it to find adjacent labels.
  • 6Write exact words from the passage — word form and spelling must be precise.
Common Mistakes
  • Labelling the wrong part because you misread the diagram structure (confusing "top" with "bottom" in a cross-section).
  • Using a general noun instead of the specific technical term used in the passage.
  • Exceeding the word limit — most commonly ONE or TWO WORDS only.
Budget 90 seconds per label. Spend 30 seconds locating the relevant passage section quickly — the rest goes to reading and extraction.
R11
Short-Answer Questions
Short
What & WhyWh-questions (what, where, who, when, how many) about specific factual details in the passage. Answers are brief — 1–3 words and/or a number, extracted directly from the text. Questions follow text order. The text is in front of you — spelling errors are inexcusable.
  • 1Read each question and identify exactly what type of information is sought — a name, a number, a place, a date, a single-word reason.
  • 2Underline the key noun or concept and use it to locate the relevant passage section.
  • 3Read that section carefully — short answers require precise extraction, not generalisation.
  • 4Write the exact word(s) from the passage. "What material is used?" → write "aluminium," not "it is made of aluminium."
  • 5Check that your answer grammatically answers the question. "When did…?" needs a date/time; "Who…?" needs a name or noun phrase.
  • 6Questions follow text order — pace yourself through the passage accordingly.
Common Mistakes
  • Writing full sentences instead of the short answer — often causes word limit violations with zero extra benefit.
  • Spelling errors on words copied directly from the passage — the text is right in front of you.
  • Using a related but incorrect nearby word — re-read the sentence to confirm the exact answer.
This is typically the fastest Reading type. Target 45–60 seconds per question. Useful for recovering time if earlier types took longer than planned.

Writing

8 task types · 60 min total · Graded on 4 criteria equally

W1
Task 1: Graph / Chart / Table Description
Task 1
What & WhyOne or more graphs (line, bar, column), charts (pie), or tables with statistical data. Write a factual, objective description of key features, trends, and comparisons. No opinions. No explanations of WHY trends occurred. Minimum 150 words. Scored on: Task Achievement, Coherence & Cohesion, Lexical Resource, Grammatical Range & Accuracy.
  • 1Spend 2–3 minutes analysing the visual before writing. Identify: the main trend(s), highest/lowest values, exceptions, and key comparisons.
  • 2Write a paraphrased introduction (1–2 sentences). NEVER copy the prompt verbatim. Rephrase: "The graph shows…" → "The line graph illustrates the changes in…"
  • 3Write an overview paragraph (2–3 sentences). THIS IS THE MOST IMPORTANT PARAGRAPH. Describe the most significant general trends WITHOUT specific figures. Think: what would a reader need to know before looking at the data?
  • 4Write 1–2 detail paragraphs grouping data logically (by category, time period, or rank). Include specific data to support each point: "rose sharply from 20% to 65% between 2010 and 2020."
  • 5Use varied trend vocabulary: increased dramatically, fell steadily, remained relatively stable, reached a peak of, dropped to a low of, fluctuated, plateaued at, accounted for X%.
  • 6Never write a conclusion — this is a description, not an essay. End after your last detail point.
  • 7Select the most significant data — you don't need every single figure. Examiners want to see you can identify what matters.
Common Mistakes
  • Omitting the overview — the most penalised omission in Task 1. Without it, Task Achievement is capped below Band 6.
  • Describing every single figure without selection — reads as a catalogue, not a description.
  • Including opinions or reasons ("I think this is because globalisation caused…") — Task 1 requires purely objective description.
20 minutes total: 2–3 min analysis → 1 min planning structure → 14–15 min writing → 2 min proofreading. Aim for 165–185 words. Going significantly over wastes time better spent on Task 2.
W2
Task 1: Process Diagram Description
Task 1
What & WhyA diagram showing a sequence of steps — natural (water cycle, life cycle) or man-made (manufacturing, water treatment) — with arrows connecting stages. No statistics or numbers. You describe all stages from start to finish in a logical sequence. EVERY stage must be covered.
  • 1Study the diagram systematically: identify the start point, end point, number of stages, and any loops, feedback cycles, or branches.
  • 2Write a paraphrased introduction: "The diagram illustrates the process by which [X is produced / X occurs], consisting of [N] main stages."
  • 3Write an overview: state whether the process is cyclical or linear, and approximately how many stages it involves.
  • 4Describe each stage in sequence using sequencing language: firstly, then, subsequently, following this, at this point, the next stage involves, finally, once [X] has occurred.
  • 5Use passive voice for man-made processes: "The raw materials are heated to 400°C before being filtered." Passive is natural and expected here.
  • 6Cover EVERY stage — unlike data tasks, you cannot select and omit. Examiners check all steps are covered.
Common Mistakes
  • Treating it like a data task and looking for trends — there are none. Describe stages and relationships only.
  • Writing in the wrong tense — use simple present (or past for historical processes). Avoid future tense.
  • Omitting stages — examiners explicitly check that the full process is covered.
20 minutes total: 2 min tracing the process end-to-end → 1 min planning → 15 min writing → 2 min review. Process diagrams rarely justify going over 200 words — precision is more valued than length.
W3
Task 1: Map Description
Task 1
What & WhyTwo maps of the same location at different times (e.g. 1980 and 2020), or a plan showing proposed changes. Describe what has changed, what remains the same, and the nature of any transformation. Requires precise spatial language and change vocabulary.
  • 1Annotate both maps before writing. Categorise every feature as: New (Map 2 only), Demolished (Map 1 only), Retained (both maps), or Modified (changed position/label).
  • 2Write a paraphrased introduction: "The maps compare the layout of [place] in [year] and [year]."
  • 3Write an overview summarising the most significant overall change: "Overall, the area underwent significant development, with residential housing largely replaced by commercial buildings."
  • 4Organise body paragraphs by zone/area (northern area, town centre, outskirts) rather than listing individual features — creates better coherence.
  • 5Use precise spatial language: in the north/south/east/west, to the left/right of, adjacent to, opposite, in the centre of, where X once stood, replaced by, expanded to include.
  • 6Use change vocabulary: was demolished, was replaced by, was constructed, remained unchanged, was extended, a new X was built, was relocated to.
  • 7Describe continuity too — things that stayed the same are important and earn marks for completeness.
Common Mistakes
  • Listing every single feature without grouping or prioritising — reads as a catalogue, not a description.
  • Using vague language like "some things changed" — be specific about which features changed and how.
  • Forgetting to describe continuity — features that remained unchanged must be noted.
20 minutes total: 3 min annotating both maps → 1 min planning → 14 min writing → 2 min proofreading. Aim for 160–185 words.
W4
Task 2: Opinion Essay (Agree / Disagree)
Task 2
What & WhyA statement expressing a point of view followed by "To what extent do you agree or disagree?" You must express a clear personal position and defend it. This is the most common Task 2 type. The examiner expects a clear, consistent stance supported by well-developed arguments — NOT a fence-sitting summary of both sides.
  • 1Decide your position BEFORE writing — fully agree, partially agree, or fully disagree. Do NOT change position mid-essay. Partial agreement (conceding one point while disagreeing overall) is valid and demonstrates sophistication.
  • 2Plan your structure (5 min): Introduction (paraphrase + clear thesis) → Body 1 (main argument + development + example) → Body 2 (second argument OR counter-argument with rebuttal) → Conclusion (restate position + summary).
  • 3Develop each body paragraph fully. A strong paragraph has: a topic sentence → 2–3 sentences of explanation/reasoning → a concrete example. Shallow paragraphs score poorly.
  • 4Use hedging language if partially agreeing: "admittedly," "it must be acknowledged that," "while it is true that X, the reality is that Y."
  • 5Avoid listing. The depth of ONE well-developed argument beats the breadth of FIVE undeveloped bullet points every time.
  • 6Write a strong conclusion. Restate your position using different vocabulary — don't just copy your introduction.
  • 7Never say "I agree to some extent" and then discuss both sides equally — that is a discussion essay, not an opinion essay.
Common Mistakes
  • Changing your position mid-essay — this catastrophically damages Task Response.
  • Writing a balanced discussion instead of taking a clear stance — this misidentifies the task type.
  • Under-developing body paragraphs with only 2–3 sentences of assertions — examiners want reasoning and examples.
40 minutes total: 5 min planning → 30 min writing → 5 min proofreading. Aim for 270–300 words. Writing more than 320 words rarely improves your score and uses time needed for proofreading.
W5
Task 2: Discussion Essay (Both Sides + Opinion)
Task 2
What & WhyPrompt usually reads: "Discuss both views and give your own opinion." You must present BOTH sides of an argument AND give your own position. Different from Opinion Essay — you are expected to genuinely explore both perspectives before concluding. Common structure: Intro → Body 1 (View A) → Body 2 (View B) → Conclusion (your opinion).
  • 1Identify the two views in the prompt BEFORE planning. Confirm you understand what "both sides" means for this specific topic.
  • 2Give approximately equal coverage to both views in your body paragraphs — balance demonstrates objectivity.
  • 3Your opinion MUST appear — typically in the introduction (briefly) AND the conclusion (fully developed). Do not hide or omit it.
  • 4Use contrast and concession language: "While proponents of X argue that…, those on the other side contend that…"
  • 5Develop each body paragraph with a topic sentence, reasoning, and a supporting example — don't list points superficially.
  • 6In the conclusion, clearly state YOUR view and briefly explain why it outweighs the opposing view.
Common Mistakes
  • Omitting your own opinion — the prompt explicitly asks for it; omitting it caps Task Response.
  • Writing only one body paragraph covering both views — this is rushed and underdeveloped.
  • Treating it as a pure Opinion Essay and ignoring the opposing view — this misidentifies the task type.
40 minutes total: 5 min planning → 30 min writing → 5 min proofreading. A four-paragraph structure (Intro + 2 Body + Conclusion) is ideal. Aim for 270–300 words.
W6
Task 2: Problem & Solution Essay
Task 2
What & WhyCommon prompt: "What are the problems associated with this? What solutions can you suggest?" Or: "What are the causes of this problem and what measures could be taken to address them?" Write a minimum 250-word essay identifying problems/causes and proposing realistic, specific solutions/measures.
  • 1Read the prompt carefully to confirm whether it asks for problems + solutions, causes + solutions, or causes + effects. These are different tasks.
  • 2Structure: Introduction (paraphrase topic + brief overview) → Body 1 (Problems/Causes — 2 developed examples) → Body 2 (Solutions/Measures — directly matched to problems) → Conclusion.
  • 3Match solutions directly to problems. If you identify "lack of funding" as a problem, your solution should address "how to increase funding" — not a generic point.
  • 4Make solutions specific and realistic. "The government should invest in education" is too vague. "Governments could allocate a higher percentage of GDP — such as 6% — to public schooling" is specific and credible.
  • 5Choose 2 problems and 2 solutions — depth of development matters more than breadth. Avoid listing 5+ underdeveloped points.
  • 6Use appropriate vocabulary: "One of the most pressing issues is…", "A key solution would be to…", "This measure would effectively address the problem of…"
Common Mistakes
  • Misidentifying the task type — confusing "causes and effects" with "problems and solutions" leads to off-topic responses.
  • Proposing solutions that don't match the problems raised — creates an incoherent essay.
  • Being too vague — "the government should do something" is not a solution; it's an evasion.
40 minutes total: 5 min planning → 30 min writing → 5 min proofreading. Plan your body paragraphs explicitly before writing — the problem-solution connection must be logical and traceable.
W7
Task 2: Advantages & Disadvantages Essay
Task 2
What & WhyTwo variants exist: (1) "Discuss the advantages and disadvantages" — no opinion required; (2) "Do the advantages outweigh the disadvantages?" — your opinion IS required. Read the prompt very carefully to know which variant you have. Structure: Intro → Body 1 (advantages) → Body 2 (disadvantages) → Conclusion.
  • 1Read the prompt twice to confirm: does it ask you to DISCUSS advantages and disadvantages (no opinion), or does it ask you to EVALUATE which outweighs the other (opinion required)?
  • 2For "outweigh" variants, state your position clearly in the introduction and reinforce it in the conclusion.
  • 3Give broadly equal coverage to both advantages and disadvantages — don't write 80% on one side and 20% on the other.
  • 4Develop 1–2 advantages and 1–2 disadvantages in depth, with reasoning and examples for each point.
  • 5Use evaluative vocabulary: "A significant benefit of this is…", "A major drawback, however, is…", "This outweighs the disadvantage because…"
  • 6In the conclusion, summarise both sides and (if required) clearly state which outweighs the other and why.
Common Mistakes
  • Giving an opinion when none is requested — or failing to give one when it IS requested. Read the prompt.
  • Heavily biasing one side — even in "outweigh" variants, you must develop both sides, just conclude one is stronger.
  • Listing advantages/disadvantages without development — "It saves time. It saves money. It saves effort." is a list, not an essay.
40 minutes total: 5 min planning → 30 min writing → 5 min proofreading. The prompt variant (discuss vs. outweigh) changes your thesis — identify it first or risk writing the wrong essay.
W8
Task 2: Double Question Essay
Task 2
What & WhyThe prompt contains TWO explicit, separate questions (e.g., "Why is this happening? Is this a positive or negative development?" or "What are the effects of this? What can individuals do to address it?"). BOTH questions must be answered with equal weight or marks will be lost for partially addressing the task.
  • 1Identify BOTH questions in the prompt and write them down before planning. It sounds obvious, but many students miss the second question under exam pressure.
  • 2Structure: Introduction (paraphrase + brief answer to both questions) → Body 1 (answering Question 1 fully) → Body 2 (answering Question 2 fully) → Conclusion.
  • 3Give roughly equal word count to each question. If Body 1 is 120 words, Body 2 should be approximately 120 words too.
  • 4Don't blend the two questions together in one body paragraph — keep them clearly separated for coherence and clarity.
  • 5In the conclusion, briefly revisit BOTH questions — don't just summarise the second one.
  • 6Use clear signposting: "Regarding the first question…", "Turning to the second issue…", "In terms of [Q1]… As for [Q2]…"
Common Mistakes
  • Only answering one of the two questions — this is extremely common and caps Task Response at Band 5 maximum.
  • Spending 80% of the essay on one question and barely touching the other.
  • Blending both answers into one muddled body paragraph — each question deserves its own, clearly structured paragraph.
40 minutes total: 5 min planning (identify BOTH questions explicitly) → 30 min writing → 5 min proofreading. Before submitting, re-read the prompt and confirm both questions are answered.

Speaking

3 parts · 11–14 min · Graded on 4 criteria: Fluency, Lexis, Grammar, Pronunciation

S1
Part 1 — Interview / Personal Questions
Part 1
What & Why4–5 minutes. The examiner asks 4–6 questions on 2–3 familiar topics (home, work/study, hobbies, food, transport, weather, technology). Questions are straightforward and personal. Responses should be conversational and natural — not a formal speech. Aim for 2–4 sentences per answer.
  • 1Use the D-E-E-P formula: Direct answer → Explain it → Example or Extension → Personal feeling. "I enjoy cooking. It's a great way to relax after work. I especially love making Italian dishes. I find the process very meditative."
  • 2Answer naturally, not formulaically. Don't start every answer with "That's a very interesting question" — examiners penalise rehearsed-sounding openers.
  • 3Avoid one-word answers ("Yes" / "No") — always develop. But don't over-talk either. 2–4 sentences is ideal per question in Part 1.
  • 4Use a range of tenses naturally. Part 1 questions about habits use present tense; questions about childhood use past tense. Don't force complex structures unnaturally.
  • 5Don't memorise scripts. Examiners can tell. Instead, practise talking fluently about familiar topics until it feels natural.
  • 6Maintain a conversational pace. Don't rush. Use natural pausing. A brief "Hmm, let me think…" is acceptable and human — it's not penalised.
Common Mistakes
  • Under-answering (one sentence only) — this doesn't give the examiner enough language to assess.
  • Over-preparing scripted answers that sound unnatural and robotic.
  • Speaking too fast due to nervousness — slowing down actually improves fluency scores.
Part 1 lasts 4–5 minutes. You have no prep time — it starts immediately. Treat it as warming up your voice and confidence. The examiner is looking for natural, spontaneous English, not perfection.
S2
Part 2 — Individual Long Turn (Cue Card)
Part 2
What & WhyYou receive a cue card with a topic and 3–4 bullet points (e.g., "Describe a person who inspired you. You should say: who this person is, how you know them, what they have done, and explain why they inspired you."). You have exactly 1 minute to prepare notes. Then speak for 1–2 minutes uninterrupted. The examiner will stop you at 2 minutes.
  • 1Use your 1-minute preparation time effectively. Write 3–5 word bullet-point notes for each cue card point — not full sentences. You speak from notes, not a script.
  • 2Cover ALL bullet points on the cue card. Examiners check that you address all listed aspects.
  • 3Structure your talk: brief introduction → address each bullet point → brief conclusion/reflection. Use signpost phrases: "So let me start by telling you about…", "Moving on to…", "And finally, the reason this person inspired me is…"
  • 4Aim to speak for the full 2 minutes. If you stop early (e.g. at 45 seconds), your score suffers. The examiner will let you know when time is up.
  • 5Use a variety of tenses. Cue cards about past events need past tense; you can add present reflection ("This experience has taught me that…") and hypothetical language ("If I could meet this person again, I would…").
  • 6Use vivid details and specific language. "A tall man with a warm smile who always arrived early to class" is far more impressive than "a good teacher."
  • 7Don't panic if you run out of ideas on a bullet point — circle back to a previous point with "And another thing I should mention about this is…"
Common Mistakes
  • Speaking for less than 1 minute — the minimum acceptable length is about 1 minute 30 seconds.
  • Writing full sentences during prep time instead of quick notes — you won't have time to read them.
  • Ignoring one or more of the cue card bullet points — all must be addressed.
1 minute prep is short. Decide immediately what person/place/event you'll talk about and jot quick notes per bullet point. Don't change your topic during preparation — you won't have time to re-plan.
S3
Part 3 — Two-Way Discussion
Part 3
What & Why4–5 minutes. A two-way conversation with the examiner on abstract, thematic topics linked to your Part 2 topic. Questions become more complex: "Why do you think…?", "Do you think society should…?", "How has this changed compared to the past?", "What might happen in the future if…?" This part tests higher-order thinking and sophisticated language use.
  • 1Give extended answers — aim for 4–6 sentences per response. This is not Part 1. Part 3 expects depth, analysis, and reasoning, not brief conversational answers.
  • 2Use the P-E-E structure: Point → Evidence/Example → Elaboration/Why. "I think technology has generally improved education. For instance, students in remote areas can now access world-class university lectures online for free. This has democratised knowledge in a way that was simply unimaginable twenty years ago."
  • 3Use speculative and analytical language: "It could be argued that…", "One possible explanation is…", "There's a strong case to be made for…", "From an economic perspective…", "In the long term, this might lead to…"
  • 4If you need thinking time, use natural delay strategies: "That's a thought-provoking question. Let me consider…", "I haven't thought about that before, but I'd say…" Avoid silence or "um, um, um."
  • 5Be willing to change your position or add nuance: "While I initially thought X, I can also see that Y has merit because…" This demonstrates sophisticated thinking.
  • 6Use a range of complex grammatical structures: conditionals ("If governments were to invest more in…"), passive constructions, relative clauses, and perfect tenses.
  • 7Don't just agree with everything. The examiner may challenge your view — maintain your position confidently or concede gracefully with reasoning.
Common Mistakes
  • Giving Part 1-length answers (1–2 sentences) — Part 3 requires depth and analysis, not brief replies.
  • Saying "I don't know" and stopping — always follow up with "but I suppose…" or "I imagine that…"
  • Avoiding all complex vocabulary — Part 3 is your main opportunity to demonstrate sophisticated lexical resource.
Part 3 is the highest-scoring opportunity in the Speaking test. The examiner expects you to lead the discussion with extended, analytical responses. Think of it as a mini academic debate — your fluency, vocabulary, and grammar complexity are all on full display.
